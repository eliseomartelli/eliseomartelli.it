When we talk about "Good Design" in software and computer science, we usually refer to the presentation aspects of the product we are analyzing, neglecting all the internal design efforts, decisions, and affordances that might've been necessary to build a said product.

People are working hard to make the next generation of communication technologies, chip designs, data centers, and whatnot. As citizens of this field, we got to pledge to leave a reasonable footprint on what we make and do.

That's the fascinating aspect of computer science: nothing is set in stone yet.

Lately, I've been taking a closer look at the great work of Dieter Rams, a famous industrial designer that worked for companies like [Braun](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Braun_(company)>) and [Vitsœ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vitsœ).
He was a great proposer of the "less but better" ideology.
You can see a testament to these words by examining his designs.
He became a figure of great interest in the design world. His influence is still resounding in contemporary works of designers all across the globe.

## The products of software and infrastructure development

I think that for each codebase or each infrastructure deployment, there are at least two interlinked products:

1. The infrastructure or the program;
2. the taken decisions to build the said product.

For me, the second point is the most important of the two. That's where design lies.

The word "Design" comes from the Latin word "designo", which assumes the meaning of describe, indicate, choose, or plan.

If we look at it from this perspective, this is something we do every day. Are there any notions we can acquire from Dieter Rams?

## Ten principles for good design

> An impenetrable confusion of forms, colours and noises.

That's how he described the world around him, and that's the base he used to enunciate these principles.

<HighlightSection text={"Innovative"} number={'1'}/>
Innovation can be of all forms and shapes. When building something, you decide what to put in and to leave out, the software/hardware stack to use, and how everything connects.

Innovation means finding new ways to use the existing toolset and building new tools to achieve the final goal.
